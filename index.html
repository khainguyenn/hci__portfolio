Design Manifesto

Introduction

As part of Davidson College’s first Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) course, I was excited to explore a new horizon in computer science. Over three transformative months, I ventured into a field previously unfamiliar to me—HCI.

Before this course, my work centered on coding functionalities, often overlooking the critical role of user-centered design. HCI expanded my perspective, emphasizing the importance of designing intuitive and accessible systems that meet users' needs. From crafting website designs and conducting user research to developing immersive VR prototypes and applying advanced data visualization techniques, this class closely mirrored professional HCI practices.

To synthesize what I’ve learned, I present this design manifesto outlining five key principles that shaped my design process. These principles aim to ensure that designs are not only functional but also useful, accessible, and deeply attuned to the needs of their end users.

POINT 1: Understand Users: The Foundation of Human-Centered Design

Lesson 1: Understanding User Personas Enhances Personalization

One of the most impactful lessons I learned in HCI was the value of user personas in guiding personalized design decisions. During the Knotty and Board website redesign, our team created three distinct personas—Priscilla, Petra, and August—each with unique needs. For example, Priscilla, a 45-year-old designer, valued efficiency and preferred curated grids of furniture sets, while Petra, a 72-year-old, needed large, high-resolution images to showcase authenticity and quality. August, a 23-year-old pet owner, appreciated prominently featured pet-friendly furniture options.

To create these personas, we used empathy mapping and needfinding methods, as outlined in The Empathy Fieldguide. This approach helped us identify pain points and tailor solutions to serve users’ needs and individual contexts effectively. The process demonstrated that personas are not static descriptions but highly practical tools to steer the design process based on user behaviors and goals.

Takeaway: Personas ensure designs are personal and relevant, bridging the gap between technical functionality and emotional engagement.

Lesson 2: Prioritizing Parameters Based on User Needs

In the Mental Health Visualization project, I learned how to prioritize data to meet diverse user needs effectively. The dataset included variables like sleep, GPA, exercise, and stress. By focusing on three key groups—educators, parents, and students—we identified what each group valued most. Educators wanted insights on stress and performance, parents focused on sleep and academic success, and students looked for actionable ways to improve well-being.

Using principles from The Design of Everyday Things , we designed intuitive, accessible visualizations, normalizing parameters into "low," "medium," and "high" categories with color-coded indicators. This iterative process, guided by defining and ideating, allowed us to create visualizations that addressed diverse needs while simplifying complex data.

Takeaway: Prioritizing user needs ensures that designs are approachable, impactful, and aligned with stakeholder goals.

POINT 2: Prioritizing and Creating Features Based on Screen Real Estate

Lesson 1: Adapting Content for Limited Real Estate (Smartwatch Design - Knotty and Board)

Designing for small screens like smartwatches requires prioritizing essential features to avoid overwhelming users. For the Knotty and Board smartwatch app, we focused on two key tasks: accessing the shopping bag and tracking orders. These practical features addressed immediate user needs and ensured simplicity.

We also utilized the smartwatch’s spinning crown for scrolling and screen switching, a critical adjustment for small screens where touch-based navigation can be challenging for users with larger hands. By aligning features with the device's hardware, we created a streamlined experience that worked seamlessly within the smartwatch’s constraints.

Takeaway: When designing for small screens, prioritize quick, essential tasks and leverage hardware features to enhance usability and avoid cluttered interfaces.

Lesson 2: Leveraging Expansive Space for Enhanced Engagement (Large Multi-Touch Display - Knotty and Board)

Large screens allow for high-quality visuals and intuitive content organization. In the Knotty and Board multi-touch display design, we divided the screen into modules: the central section showcased product images in a swipeable carousel, while sidebars housed customer testimonials and add-on deals. This layout utilized the space effectively, balancing engagement with clarity.

In our Design for Another World project, we extended these principles to VR, integrating 4D visualization that combined spatial, temporal, and interactive elements. For the VR Meditation Room, each environment (desert, forest, mountain) focused on soothing visuals and complementary ambient sounds, creating a fully immersive experience. Compared to traditional flat-screen designs, VR required rethinking layout and sensory feedback to prioritize engagement without overwhelming users.

Takeaway: For large or immersive displays, leverage space creatively to highlight key content and balance complexity with simplicity to maintain usability and engagement.

POINT 3: Carefully Consider Tradeoffs When Making Design Decisions

Lesson 1: Prioritize Simplicity to Maintain Usability

A key insight from the VR Meditation Room project was the importance of simplicity in interaction design. Initially, we planned to use a single-button navigation system, where all actions—selection, navigation, and confirmation—are controlled by a single input. While innovative, this approach proved impractical on mobile VR platforms like Google Cardboard, which lacked precise input capabilities. According to Don Norman in The Design of Everyday Things, simplifying interactions can reduce cognitive load and improve usability, particularly for novice users.

To address this, we pivoted to a gaze-based navigation system, allowing users to interact with the virtual environment simply by focusing on objects. This adjustment removed unnecessary complexity and ensured the experience was intuitive and accessible, especially for first-time VR users.

Takeaway: Prioritizing simplicity over advanced functionality creates more inclusive designs and lowers barriers to entry for new users.

Lesson 2: Balance Functionality with Constraints for Feasibility

In the Mental Health Visualization project, we initially envisioned an intricate, multidimensional model to represent the relationships between CGPA, sleep, exercise, and mental health. However, the complexity risked overwhelming users and exceeded our technical and time resources.

Instead, we simplified the visualization by normalizing data to a 0–10 scale and using color-coded blocks to represent dimensions. This design focused on clarity while retaining key insights, striking a balance between ambition and feasibility. The result was a model that delivered meaningful information in a user-friendly format.

Takeaway: Simplifying a design while maintaining its core functionality ensures usability and keeps the project within scope, especially under constraints.

Both projects highlighted the importance of balancing functionality and usability by carefully weighing tradeoffs. By prioritizing essential features and adapting to technical constraints, we created designs that remained meaningful and accessible. This strategic focus on simplicity allowed us to deliver high-quality user experiences without compromising clarity or effectiveness.

POINT 4: Always Consider Accessibility

Accessibility in design is more than a technical checkbox—it is a commitment to inclusivity and empathy. My HCI experiences have emphasized the importance of designing for all users, ensuring that technology remains a tool for empowerment rather than a source of exclusion. From physical prototypes to virtual interfaces, accessibility considerations shaped every aspect of my design journey.

In the Re-Design and Extend: Making Mental Health Tangible project, we developed an analytical physicalization to represent relationships between college students' mental health factors, including GPA, sleep, exercise, and financial stress. Initially, our LEGO-based prototype provided a tactile way to interact with data. However, feedback revealed shortcomings in accessibility and usability. For example, color choices, like purple cubes, presented challenges for users with color blindness. Addressing this, we adopted high-contrast primary colors—red, green, blue, and yellow—and added tactile markers to accommodate users with visual impairments.

However, later on, we realized that even this combination of colors posed challenges. While red, green, blue, and yellow addressed certain types of color blindness, they created new barriers for others. This limitation underscored the complexity of designing for universal accessibility. Drawing from insights in Disability Studies as a Source of Critical Inquiry for the Field of Assistive Technology by Mankoff et al., we reflected on the societal implications of design choices. The paper highlights how both social and medical models of disability shape the experiences of users and emphasizes the importance of avoiding "one-size-fits-all" solutions. It was a humbling realization: no single design choice could fully accommodate everyone. Despite these constraints, our goal remained to make the visualization as inclusive as possible for the largest number of users. This experience reinforced the importance of acknowledging limitations while striving for continual improvement, rather than seeking an unattainable perfection.

Ultimately, the project taught us that accessibility is a shared responsibility. It is about balancing ambition with pragmatism and always asking how we can do better for the people we aim to serve. It’s an iterative process where progress, however incremental, is a victory for inclusivity.

POINT 5: Adaptability When Expectations Differ from Reality

Learning to Pivot Through User Feedback

In the Re-Design and Extend: Making Mental Health Tangible project, our initial design featured LEGO blocks. Each stack represented a variable, and we believed users would intuitively grasp the data relationships by physically engaging with the blocks. During testing, however, it became clear the design wasn’t working as intended. Participants found it hard to understand how the variables were connected. The blocks were engaging, but the relationships felt fragmented. I realized that while we prioritized interactivity, we had overlooked the complexity of the data and how it needed to be presented.

This moment was humbling but also pivotal. Instead of clinging to the original concept, we embraced the feedback and reimagined the design. We shifted to a four-sided platform where each edge represented a variable, allowing stackable blocks to demonstrate connections from multiple perspectives.

Takeaway: Reflecting on this process, I realized that feedback is not just a list of things to fix—it’s an opportunity to rethink and improve. While it was challenging to let go of the original design, doing so opened the door to a solution that better aligned with user needs.

Conclusion: Designing with Empathy and Adaptability

My journey in Davidson College’s first Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) course taught me that great design stems from understanding people and embracing change. The principles I’ve outlined reshaped how I approach problem-solving and innovation:

  1. Understand Users: The foundation of human-centered design starts with deeply understanding user needs and personas.
  2. Prioritize and Create Features Based on Screen Real Estate: Effective designs utilize the strengths and limitations of the medium, from small smartwatches to large multi-touch displays.
  3. Carefully Consider Tradeoffs When Making Design Decisions: Balancing simplicity and functionality ensures usability while staying within constraints.
  4. Always Consider Accessibility: Inclusive design empowers all users by addressing diverse needs and limitations.
  5. Adaptability When Expectations Differ from Reality: Embracing feedback and pivoting design approaches lead to better outcomes.

Moving forward, I’m inspired to create designs that are inclusive, impactful, and centered on real human needs.
